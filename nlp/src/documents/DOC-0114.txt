# Central Haven Food Market Reopens After Renovation with Expanded Aquaculture Wing

**Haven Municipal Bulletin | 76-08-20 PCE (2118-08-20 CE)**

---

After six months of closures, scaffolding, and the persistent smell of fresh concrete drifting through Central Haven's civic quarter, the Central Haven Food Market has thrown open its doors again — and by most accounts, it was worth the wait.

The market, one of Haven's oldest continuously operating commercial institutions, first welcomed vendors and shoppers in 12 PCE (2054 CE), when Central Haven was still a patchwork of prefabricated civic blocks and the island's population was a fraction of what it is today. Sixty-four years on, the market has seen renovations before, but none quite on this scale.

The 2.3 million Phi Credits overhaul — funded in full by the CGC Civic Infrastructure Fund, not by any private investment arrangement — introduced sweeping structural upgrades to the market's interior colonnades, replaced the aging overhead ventilation systems with a modern climate-controlled array, and expanded the eastern wing into previously unused civic land to house what is already being described as the market's most significant new feature: a dedicated aquaculture wing.

"This is a market that belongs to every Havenite," said Doro Fessel, the market's director since 73 PCE, at a ribbon-cutting ceremony attended by several hundred residents on opening morning. "What we've tried to do with this renovation is make sure that when you walk in here, you see Haven's future as clearly as you see its history."

---

**Inside the New Wing**

The aquaculture wing occupies the market's entire new eastern expansion and contains 34 vendor stalls specializing in fresh and processed aquaculture products, ranging from whole protein fillets to vacuum-sealed processed blocks, fermented preparations, and ready-to-eat street food.

The stalls vary considerably in scale and style. At one end of the wing, larger vendors offer bulk protein blocks in standardized weights — the kind of staple purchasing that feeds thousands of Haven households weekly. Toward the wing's interior plaza, smaller specialty stalls have set up displays of smoked and pickled preparations, including several kelp noodle vendors whose opening-day queues stretched back well into the main market corridor.

On supply, the wing draws from two primary sources. Zonnon Island protein farms — the vast offshore installations whose scale is, by most accounts, visible from orbit — supply approximately 40% of the aquaculture wing's fresh stock, a continuation and formalization of the informal trade relationships that have long made Zonnon a quiet but essential contributor to Haven's food supply. The remaining primary stock comes from Cyanite Industries' seabed kelp harvesting operations, which work the mineral-rich waters north of Haven Island and have become an increasingly significant food-supply contributor alongside the company's better-known industrial activities.

---

**A Market for 15,000**

On a typical day, the Central Haven Food Market receives an estimated 15,000 visitors — a figure that market management expects the new aquaculture wing to help sustain through the midday hours, when foot traffic has historically dipped.

The CGC Civic Infrastructure Fund, which approved the renovation budget earlier this year, has positioned the project as part of a broader commitment to Central Haven's public commercial spaces. For most Havenites who turned up on reopening day, however, the politics of funding were a distant second to the more immediate question of whether the kelp noodle stall near the east entrance would run out before they reached the front.

Based on opening morning, the answer was: barely.

---

*The Central Haven Food Market is open daily, 06:00–20:00. Full vendor listings are available at the Central Haven Civic Services kiosk.*
