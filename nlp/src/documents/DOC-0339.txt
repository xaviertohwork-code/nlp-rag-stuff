# Personal Journal Extract
**Field Technician — Fabrication Yard 9, Sarento Outer Industrial Ring**
**Classification: GREEN**

---

## 76-10-17

Start of another rotation. Fourteen hours on the floor at Yard 9, same as always. Whistle at first light, whistle at dark, eat something gray in between. The new conveyor manifold we spent two weeks installing threw a calibration fault three hours into the shift. Spent the rest of the day watching repair bots crawl over our work while the foreman logged the downtime against our section. Not against himself, naturally.

Walked the perimeter on break because the break room smells like hydraulic fluid and regret. The patrol units were doing their rounds out by the fence — these big blue painted things, shoulder-height, moving with that particular mechanical patience that has no interest in you unless you step wrong. Wampa's grandchildren, painted Cyanite blue. Supposedly our protection. Mostly they protect the fence from us.

---

## 76-10-22

Woke up at around the third hour to what sounded like muffled thunder from the northeast. Took me a few seconds to place it. Artillery. Second time this month the Canians have been probing out that direction. You'd think the sound would be frightening but mostly it's just annoying — I had four hours left to sleep and I lay there doing the math on whether it was getting closer or staying put. Staying put. Back to sleep eventually.

Eighteen square meters, and I can't sleep through distant artillery. Block Residence living at its finest. They gave us these units like it was a benefit. Poured concrete, a fold-down bed, a hotplate, one window that faces another identical window six meters away. I've started leaving a boot against the door just so there's something in the room that belongs to me.

---

## 76-10-29

Third artillery exchange this month, northeast again. Different timbre this time — closer or heavier, hard to say. The overnight crew said it lit up the horizon for a few minutes. I missed it. Sleeping like the dead, finally, and then I'm told I missed the light show.

Fourteen hours again today. Fourteen hours tomorrow. Fourteen hours until I die or the rotation ends, whichever.

---

## 76-11-04

Pay processed. Four thousand two hundred Phi, same as every month, and before I've touched a credit they've taken back two thousand eight hundred of it. Housing fee, meal plan, equipment and safety gear maintenance — all itemized in the automated deduction notice like they're doing me a favor by telling me what they're taking. What's left barely covers anything outside their own systems. There's no canteen that doesn't take Cyanite meal credits, no market that isn't running on Cyanite-managed stock.

I've heard people complain about the deductions being arbitrary, opaque, designed to keep you dependent. They're right to complain. It's a system built to keep your head exactly high enough that you don't drown, and exactly low enough that you can't swim anywhere else. There's nothing fair about it and everyone here knows it.

But there's nowhere else to go that pays at all.

That's the part that sits. Not the noise from the northeast, not the robots and their blue paint, not the eighteen square meters. Just that sentence, which I keep writing out in my head on the long hours of the shift, and which keeps coming out the same every time.

Yard 9, morning whistle, fourteen hours. Same tomorrow.
