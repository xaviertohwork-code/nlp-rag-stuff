# CYANITE DIRECTOR-GENERAL DEFENDS FLOODWALL BUDGET AS HAVEN'S "NON-NEGOTIABLE LIFELINE"

**Oyelaran faces committee scrutiny over 14% levy increase request; TEC representative presses on administrative overhead**

*Jael Ortiz-Dawan, Renhwa Media Haven Bureau | 76-08-22*

---

Cyanite Industries Director-General Takeshi Oyelaran appeared before the CGC Infrastructure and Public Works Committee on 76-08-20 to defend a proposed Floodwall Maintenance Levy contribution of 2.15 billion Phi Credits from CGC member assessments — a 14% increase over the prior two-year Levy cycle of approximately 1.89 billion — and ran into the kind of pointed, skeptical questioning that his company's budget requests have not always attracted in more collegial committee sessions.

The Levy is a dedicated biennial assessment ring-fenced specifically for Floodwall maintenance and operations, distinct from the broader CGC capital expenditure collections, which are wider in scope and significantly larger in aggregate. What Cyanite is asking for from this particular line item is, in isolation, a fraction of what flows through the CGC's capital machinery — but the fraction has grown, and at least two committee members made clear that they had noticed.

---

## Testimony: "A Function No Other Corporation Could Replicate"

Oyelaran, who has led Cyanite's executive functions for 17 years since taking the Director-General post in 59 PCE, arrived in his characteristic no-ceremony fashion: a single aide, no prepared visual materials, and remarks that dispensed with pleasantries inside the first ninety seconds.

"The Floodwalls are not a line item. They are the reason Haven exists," Oyelaran told the committee. "We maintain them as a civic function. There is no other megacorporation on this council with the industrial capacity, the materials expertise, or the infrastructure reach to replicate what Cyanite does every day to keep this island dry."

The technical reality underlying that claim is difficult to dispute. The Floodwall system currently holds back waters estimated at 14 meters above Haven Island's average ground elevation — a figure Oyelaran's team included in the submitted budget dossier and which no committee member moved to contest. Without sustained maintenance, engineers have long assessed that the system's redundancy margins would erode within years, not decades.

Oyelaran attributed the bulk of the 14% levy increase to three factors: accelerating corrosion rates in the northern wall's older retaining channel infrastructure, a scheduled overhaul of nuclear fission pump units powering the primary drainage systems, and updated materials pricing reflective of the current extraction environment in Cyanite's supply chain.

---

## The TEC Challenge

The sharpest exchange of the session came not from one of the Old 3's familiar critics but from TEC's committee representative, who questioned — in language that was pointed without being hostile — whether Cyanite's cost projections included unnecessary administrative overhead.

For the avoidance of doubt, it was a TEC representative who raised the challenge, not a Renhwa Media affiliate or any other party with a longstanding editorial interest in Cyanite's operations.

Oyelaran's response was blunt: "If you want to audit the engineering teams running the northern wall pump overhaul, I'll arrange the visit. What I won't do is stand here and agree that keeping Haven's most critical infrastructure operational from one cycle to the next is an administrative inconvenience."

The TEC representative did not press further, and the exchange concluded without a formal request for supplemental documentation — though committee rules allow members to file such requests within ten business days of a hearing.

---

## Analyst Reaction

Independent infrastructure analysts contacted by Renhwa Media offered a mixed read. The 14% increase is large relative to historical Levy growth, which has averaged closer to 7–9% per cycle, but several noted that deferred maintenance from earlier cycles — a pattern that prior committee sessions had flagged without consequence — may have concentrated costs into the current request.

"Cyanite has a point about the civic function argument, but that argument is also conveniently unchallengeable," one analyst said, speaking without attribution. "No one is going to vote to underfund the Floodwalls. That's precisely why the budget scrutiny matters — it's the only moment of accountability that exists."

A vote on the Levy contribution is expected before the close of the 76 PCE fiscal calendar.

---

## SIDEBAR: Takeshi Oyelaran — 17 Years at the Helm

Takeshi Oyelaran has served as Director-General of Cyanite Industries since 59 PCE, a tenure that makes him the longest-serving chief executive of any current CGC megacorporation. A third-generation Cyanite executive, he is publicly associated with the company's "builders" identity — the positioning of Cyanite as the organization that keeps Clairos physically functional while others deal in finance, media, and software. Critics characterize that identity as a convenient deflection from the company's considerable political activity within CGC bodies. His 17-year tenure is unusual by any standard; succession questions within Cyanite's senior ranks have circulated for years without apparent resolution.
