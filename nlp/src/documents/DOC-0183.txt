# Marine Ecology After the Cascade: Sixty-Year Survey of Aquatic Biodiversity Around Haven Island

**Haven Institute of Environmental Sciences**
**Dr. Lena Marstova and Dr. Obi Ferrante**
Classification: OPEN
Date: 76-08-20 PCE / 2118-08-20 CE

---

## Abstract

This paper presents findings from a comprehensive sixty-year longitudinal survey of marine biodiversity in the waters surrounding Haven Island, spanning 16 PCE to 76 PCE. The survey draws on historical baseline data compiled from pre-Cascade coastal surveys, supplemented by systematic sampling conducted at three-year intervals across sixteen designated monitoring stations within a 30-kilometer radius of Haven Island. Our headline biodiversity metric — fish species diversity within 10 kilometers of Haven Island — shows a 41% decline since the Cascade. We further document 14 marine species that have achieved local extinction in Haven's surrounding waters. Secondary findings address the chemical and physical drivers of these changes, including the contribution of Floodwall effluent and industrial discharge to observed salinity gradients and trace isotope accumulation. The findings carry implications for ongoing environmental governance and for industrial operators whose discharge activities affect the marine environment.

---

## 1. Introduction

The Cascade of 2042 CE (Year 0 PCE) fundamentally and irreversibly altered the marine environments of Clairos. The sudden influx of vast volumes of freshwater, the displacement and death of enormous quantities of coastal biomass, and the disruption of established current patterns combined to produce ecological shocks whose downstream effects are still unfolding eight decades later. Haven Island, created through extensive post-Cascade land reclamation efforts and subsequently enclosed by the Floodwall system, represents a particularly acute site of anthropogenic marine pressure: it is, in effect, an artificial island sustained in a below-sea-level basin by continuous mechanical intervention, discharging large volumes of processed water into the surrounding marine environment on an uninterrupted basis.

Prior surveys of Haven Island's marine ecology have addressed specific questions — the 47 PCE Census of Benthic Communities, the 52 PCE Thermal Gradient Survey — but no study has yet attempted a unified longitudinal analysis spanning the full post-Cascade period. The present survey addresses this gap. We emphasize that our headline metric is fish species diversity, not coral reef coverage or other structural habitat indicators, which we treat as supplementary variables. Fish species diversity provides a more tractable, data-dense longitudinal signal and serves as a reliable integrative indicator of broader ecosystem health across the full survey period.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Survey Period and Baseline

The study covers 60 years of ecological data from 16 PCE to 76 PCE (2058 CE to 2118 CE). The 16 PCE start date was selected to coincide with the earliest systematic post-Cascade biological survey of Haven Island's surrounding waters, which was conducted by predecessor institutions to the Haven Institute of Environmental Sciences during the first phases of land reclamation. Pre-Cascade baseline figures for fish species diversity and community composition are derived from the Canian Coastal Resource Archive, which maintained continuous ichthyological survey data from 2019 CE through 2041 CE.

### 2.2 Sampling Design

Sixteen permanent monitoring stations were established at the survey's commencement, arranged in three concentric rings: six stations within 5 kilometers of Haven Island's Floodwall outfall points, six stations at 10-kilometer distances, and four outer reference stations at 25–30 kilometers. Stations were sampled using standardized trawl and seine net protocols at three-year intervals, supplemented by continuous passive acoustic monitoring since 43 PCE and water chemistry sampling since 31 PCE. A total of 22 sampling campaigns were conducted over the survey period.

### 2.3 Water Chemistry Analysis

Water samples collected at each station were analyzed for salinity, dissolved oxygen, pH, heavy metal concentrations, and trace isotope content. Tritium analysis was incorporated into the standard sampling protocol from 38 PCE onward, following early indications of anomalous isotope profiles in stations proximate to Floodwall primary outfall zones.

## 3. Findings

### 3.1 Fish Species Diversity: Headline Biodiversity Metric

Fish species diversity within 10 kilometers of Haven Island has declined by 41% since the Cascade. This figure, derived by comparing the 2041 CE Canian Coastal Resource Archive baseline against our 76 PCE sampling campaign, represents the study's primary biodiversity metric. The decline is not uniformly distributed: stations within 5 kilometers of the primary Floodwall outfall zones show a 54% reduction in species richness, while outer reference stations show a comparatively modest 18% reduction. The temporal pattern of decline is notably non-linear, with the sharpest period of reduction occurring between 16 PCE and 28 PCE (the initial land reclamation and Floodwall commissioning period), a secondary acceleration between 42 PCE and 51 PCE, and a partial stabilization in the most recent monitoring decade, which we attribute to community restructuring rather than recovery.

Commercially significant species — pelagic schooling fish, demersal flatfish, and migratory species — account for a disproportionate share of the lost diversity, with offshore and deepwater species displaying greater resilience. This pattern is consistent with the primary stressors we identify in subsequent sections, which are most intense in the nearshore environment.

### 3.2 Local Extinctions

The study identifies 14 marine species that have become locally extinct around Haven Island since the Cascade. These species span five taxonomic classes: four previously resident reef-associated fish species, three elasmobranch species (including two species of smaller coastal shark), two species of bivalve previously common to Haven Island's nearshore sediments, two cephalopod species, two crustacean species, and one species of seagrass (included here as it supports obligate marine fauna). For nine of the fourteen species, population decline correlates directly with the periods of greatest Floodwall outfall volume and land reclamation activity. For the remaining five, decline trajectories suggest a combination of Cascade-related habitat loss and continued post-settlement pressure, precluding attribution to a single driver.

### 3.3 Floodwall Effluent: Tritium and Thermal Loading

Water chemistry analysis at Floodwall proximate stations has identified consistently elevated concentrations of tritium in outfall-adjacent waters since the earliest tritium-monitoring campaigns. Tritium is a low-energy beta emitter produced as a byproduct of the nuclear fission reactors that power the Floodwall's water pumping infrastructure. Our measured concentrations range from 1.4 to 3.1 times background reference values at outfall-proximate stations, declining to background levels at the 10-kilometer monitoring ring. The study characterizes these tritium concentrations as concerning but sub-lethal for most marine vertebrates. We note that sub-lethal designations are defined against acute toxicity thresholds and do not preclude chronic sub-clinical effects on reproductive success or immune function, which remain inadequately studied in the context of Haven Island's specific marine community.

Thermal loading from outfall discharge has also measurably altered the temperature profile of nearshore waters, contributing to the observed decline in cold-water-adapted species at the innermost monitoring stations.

### 3.4 Salinity Gradients and Industrial Discharge

Cyanite Industries' reverse osmosis discharge — a byproduct of the Floodwall's function as both a water management system and a potable water production facility — is identified as a primary contributor to localized salinity gradients in Haven Island's surrounding waters. Reverse osmosis processes concentrate dissolved salts in the reject brine stream, which is discharged alongside the outfall effluent. Our measurements show salinity deviations of 2.3 to 4.8 parts per thousand above ambient at the innermost monitoring stations, with a persistent plume structure detectable at 7 kilometers under prevailing current conditions. Species abundance mapping shows strong inverse correlations between salinity deviation and the density of osmoregulatory-sensitive taxa, including juvenile fish of commercially important species, which disproportionately use nearshore nursery habitat.

Heavy metal concentrations, attributed to industrial runoff from Haven Island's port districts and from cross-bay transport from Cyanite's northern shore operations, are elevated at three monitoring stations and represent a secondary but non-negligible contributor to observed diversity decline.

## 4. Discussion

The 41% decline in fish species diversity within 10 kilometers of Haven Island since the Cascade reflects the cumulative pressure of multiple interacting stressors: the initial ecological disruption of the Cascade itself, followed by the sustained anthropogenic impacts of land reclamation, Floodwall operation, and industrial discharge. Disentangling these contributions with precision is not fully possible given the absence of a control site subject to equivalent Cascade disruption without subsequent industrial development; the outer reference stations provide the closest available comparator, but they are not equivalent.

The 14 locally extinct species represent irreversible losses from Haven Island's marine community. Where these species performed specific ecological functions — predation, bioturbation, grazing — their loss has restructured community dynamics in ways that continue to unfold. The partial stabilization in species richness observed in the most recent monitoring decade may reflect the community reaching a new, impoverished equilibrium rather than the early stages of recovery.

The chemical findings present the most direct implications for industrial management. Cyanite Industries' reverse osmosis discharge is not merely an incidental byproduct but a structurally necessary consequence of the Floodwall's operation as a desalination system; reducing brine discharge would require either a redesign of the desalination process or alternative discharge management. Neither option is addressed in any current CGC regulatory framework. The tritium findings are similarly structural: they arise from the reactor technology chosen for Floodwall power generation and cannot be remediated without changes to that technology. We present these observations as scientific characterization of causal relationships, not as advocacy for any specific remediation pathway.
