# Park's Knife: Inside Renhwa Media's Brutal First-Year Restructuring

**Haven Economic Ledger** | 74-03-11 PCE

---

When Park Soo-Hyun stepped into the CEO role at Renhwa Media in late 72 PCE, the company had spent three years bleeding market share and executive goodwill in roughly equal measure. Fourteen months later, it is leaner, quieter, and considerably smaller. Whether it is healthier is a question the industry is still arguing about.

In her first full fiscal year, Park oversaw the elimination of approximately 2,200 positions across Renhwa's content, distribution, and administrative divisions — a figure that represents close to a third of the company's peak workforce. The cuts came in three tranches, each announced with minimal preamble and executed within compressed timelines that left little room for negotiation or appeal. By the time the third wave landed in mid-73 PCE, entire editorial verticals had been dissolved, several long-running production units consolidated or shuttered, and the organizational chart reduced from a structure that once required a dedicated internal directory to one that fits, by most accounts, on a single page.

The asset disposals have been equally unsentimental. Most visibly, Renhwa sold its secondary production studio on the east Haven waterfront — a facility that had been home to several of the company's highest-profile live programming operations — for 120 million Phi Credits. The buyer has not been publicly named; the sale was confirmed in a terse regulatory filing. Several smaller licensing agreements and content archive parcels have also changed hands, though Renhwa has declined to disclose aggregate proceeds from these transactions.

---

**"Ruthless but Necessary"**

Among those who lived through the restructuring, accounts vary sharply in tone if not in their basic description of events.

"She came in and did what the board had been too afraid to do for years," said Orsolya Thenen, a former senior producer who spent eleven years at Renhwa before her position was eliminated in the second tranche. "I lost my job and I still think it was ruthless but necessary. The company was running on nostalgia and a balance sheet that didn't match."

Not everyone has reached the same accommodation with the outcome. Daven Nwaru, a former long-form documentary editor who was part of the third round of cuts, described the experience in less measured terms. "What Park did was a betrayal of Renhwa's creative legacy," he said. "The people she cut weren't inefficiencies — they were the institutional memory of what made this company worth saving. You can't run an entertainment company the way you run a freight depot."

That last image resonates in a particular way with those who have studied Park's career trajectory. Before arriving at Renhwa, she spent nearly a decade running a Sharpsea Bloc logistics firm, an experience that industry observers say is unmistakably visible in her methods. "Her background isn't in content — it's in throughput," said one financial analyst who covers the Haven entertainment sector and asked not to be named. "She treats content as a supply chain problem rather than an art problem. Get the product specified, get it made at acceptable cost, get it delivered. Whether that translates to a sustainable media business is genuinely unclear."

---

**The Statement**

Requests for comment submitted to Renhwa Media's communications office went unanswered for eleven days. Park declined to comment for this article, ultimately issuing a one-sentence written statement through a company spokesperson: *"The results speak for themselves."*

She did not hold a press conference, did not offer an interview, and did not arrange for any executive deputy to speak on her behalf. The statement stands as the entirety of Renhwa's official engagement with questions about the restructuring's scope and human toll.

Whether the results do, in fact, speak for themselves depends on which results one is reading. Renhwa's cost structure has measurably improved. Its content output has contracted. Its creative reputation, harder to quantify, is a subject on which former employees and financial analysts appear to be watching entirely different companies.

The next fiscal year will test whether the knife Park wielded was a surgeon's or a butcher's. The scars, either way, are already permanent.
