### I. BACKGROUND AND SCOPE OF INQUIRY

The CGC Compliance Secretariat has opened a formal inquiry into nanobot readings detected at Opting Clinic 7, Central Haven, on 75-11-18. The inquiry letter requests a written response within thirty days and reserves the right to convene a formal evidentiary session if the initial response is deemed insufficient. This advisory sets out recommended response strategy.

The clinic in question is operated by Genesis Labs as part of their Opting rehabilitation clinic network. That fact is material to our strategic calculus and is addressed below.

The penalties applicable under the CGC's nanobot deployment compliance framework carry graduated financial exposure beginning at 200 million Phi Credits for first-offense findings. This office does not believe that exposure to be probable at this stage; it is, however, material to the seriousness with which we are treating this matter.

---

### II. TEC'S SUBSTANTIVE POSITION

Our recommended public and regulatory position is as follows: the nanobot readings recorded at Opting Clinic 7 on 75-11-18 are consistent with residual nanobot presence in recently-Opted individuals attending the clinic. Opting is a process, not an instantaneous event. Individuals who have recently undergone Opting may retain measurable residual nanobot concentrations for a period of days to weeks following treatment, particularly in neurological proximity zones. Detection of nanobot activity at an Opting clinic premises is, accordingly, an expected and medically explicable outcome — not evidence of active external deployment.

This position is technically defensible, commercially coherent, and carries the further advantage of not requiring TEC to characterize the nature of the clinical environment or Genesis Labs' operational practices in any detail.

---

### III. INTERNAL EVIDENTIARY STATUS

This section is for internal advisement only and must not appear in any externally submitted document. TEC's monitoring logs from 75-11-18 are inconclusive as to whether the detected readings reflect active deployment versus residual presence.

This office advises that the ambiguity of the internal record is as much an asset as a liability: it cannot be used against us in a compliance proceeding, and it does not obligate us to concede any fact we have not already conceded. We recommend that any internal documentation relating to these logs be consolidated under Legal's discovery hold and treated as attorney-work-product pending resolution.

---

### IV. ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY AND GENESIS LABS RISK

TEC Legal recommends cooperative engagement with the CGC inquiry: timely response, substantive (if carefully bounded) explanation of the residual presence hypothesis, and a proactive offer to provide technical documentation supporting that explanation. We should not be seen as obstructing a compliance process in which we have a defensible substantive position.

We must, however, proceed carefully with respect to Genesis Labs. The Opting clinics are a Genesis operation. Any escalation of this inquiry — whether by TEC's non-cooperation, by a contentious CGC session, or by a leaked internal document — risks drawing Genesis Labs into the proceeding. Genesis Labs has not admitted to operating nanobots in their clinic premises, and their public posture has consistently been one of opposition to nanobot technology deployment. Implicating them in an active nanobot-presence finding, even incidentally, would be strategically counterproductive and would almost certainly damage the working relationship between TEC and CGC's broader regulatory infrastructure at a time when we can ill afford additional friction.

The recommended outcome: close this inquiry at the initial written-response stage without a formal evidentiary session, without any admission of active deployment, and without triggering Genesis Labs' involvement.
