## Central Haven Co-Housing Communities See Surge in Demand

*Wait lists climb as more Havenites look to the city's neutral core for affordable, community-driven living*

**By Lise Rochemont**

---

The numbers are hard to ignore. Wait lists for co-housing arrangements in central Haven have grown by 40% over the past 18 months, according to figures compiled by the Central Haven Residential Coordination Office — a surge that housing advocates and community managers say reflects something deeper than a simple squeeze on supply.

Central Haven is formally designated as neutral territory, administered collectively by the Clairos Governance Council rather than falling under the operational purview of any single megacorporation. That distinction, long treated as little more than an administrative technicality, appears to be acquiring genuine social weight.

"There's a waiting list now," said Orla Fennec, 34, a former logistics coordinator who moved into a co-housing unit on Arren Street eight months ago. "When I applied, I thought I'd be waiting a few weeks. It was closer to five months. But I'd do it again."

Fennec is among a growing cohort of residents — many of them Opted, though certainly not exclusively — who have come to see co-housing in the central district as something qualitatively different from accommodation available in Haven's corporate sectors. Opted residents, who have chosen to have their Edge nanobots purged, have been drawn to central Haven co-housing in particular, seeking community structures that exist outside the day-to-day architecture of megacorporation life.

"It sounds simple," Fennec added, "but this is the only place in Haven where your address doesn't advertise your corporate sponsor."

The financial calculus is not uncomplicated. Average monthly rent in central Haven co-housing runs to approximately 2,800 Credits — a figure that, while not negligible, sits well below the 4,500 Credits typical of standard sector housing across Haven's corporate districts. Residents note that co-housing units also tend to include shared amenities — communal kitchens, laundry, and common rooms — that partially offset the cost of more modest private square footage.

"It's a trade-off," acknowledged Piet Solander, 41, who shares a four-person unit near the Civic Services corridor with three other long-term residents. "You have less of your own space. But what you gain is people who actually look out for you. I'm not nobody's quarterly retention statistic here."

Solander, who describes himself as politically unaffiliated, said he first learned about co-housing through word of mouth in the neighborhood around Arren Street. He's noticed, however, that the community has grown more diverse in recent years — not just Opted residents, but workers from across multiple sectors who have simply grown weary of the implicit obligations that come with corporate-affiliated housing.

That broadening appeal has attracted the attention of organized advocacy groups. Organizers affiliated with We The Opted — none of whom agreed to be named for this article — have in recent months been openly promoting co-housing in central Haven as a form of practical political resistance, circulating materials that frame the choice of address as a meaningful act of autonomy. Whether or not residents self-identify with that framing, the numbers suggest the message is landing somewhere.

"I don't think of myself as making a political statement," said Dara Koss, 28, a graphic technician currently on the Arren Street complex's co-housing management committee. "I just wanted to live somewhere that felt like it belonged to the people in it. That turns out to be harder to find than you'd expect."

The Central Haven Residential Coordination Office did not respond to a request for comment by the time of publication. A spokesperson for the CGC Civic Services division noted only that demand for centrally administered housing had "exceeded projected capacity thresholds" and that an expansion review was under discussion.

How long that review will take — and whether supply can keep pace with a wait list already 40% larger than it was a year and a half ago — remains an open question for the hundreds of Havenites currently in the queue.
