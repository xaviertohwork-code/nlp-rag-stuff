## Edge Atmospheric Nanobot Density Monitoring
### Annual Report Summary — 76 PCE

---

### 1. Executive Summary

This report presents the Environmental Integration Division's annual summary of atmospheric nanobot density measurements across all Haven sectors for the operational year 76 PCE. Measurements were recorded via TEC's integrated environmental telemetry mesh at 4,800 fixed monitoring points distributed across Haven's inhabited and transit zones, supplemented by 214 mobile sampling units.

**The Haven-wide average atmospheric nanobot density for 76 PCE was 847 nanobots per cubic meter of air.** This represents an increase of 6.3% relative to the 75 PCE average, consistent with ongoing Edge platform user-base growth and increased commercial-sector nanobot activity. The year-on-year trend is within projected modeling parameters and does not indicate anomalous dispersal behavior.

Containment performance at Floodwall boundary points remained operationally robust. Full containment analysis is presented in Section 4.

---

**Notes on Central Haven:** Central Haven records the highest atmospheric concentrations in Haven at 2,400 to 3,100 nanobots per cubic meter. This is attributable to the sector's combined population density and mixed residential-commercial land use pattern, which generates a sustained and layered nanobot activation profile throughout the diurnal cycle. Central Haven's civic services concentration, co-housing density, and foot-traffic volume through CGC-administered public spaces all contribute to peak readings at the upper end of the recorded range during business hours.

**Notes on West Haven:** The elevated year-on-year increase in West Haven is partially attributable to expanded commercial shipping terminal activity at Haven's primary port facilities, with associated increases in nanobot activation from inbound and outbound personnel transit. This pattern has operational significance discussed further in Section 4.2.

### 3. Environmental Persistence Classification

Nanobots operating within Haven's relay network exhibit normal operational behavior at all recorded density levels. Of relevance to containment analysis is the behavior of nanobots that exit the relay network's effective range.

**TEC's formal classification of environmental persistence for relay-disconnected nanobots is non-concern status**, based on the following operationally verified behavioral profile: nanobots that lose relay connectivity enter a low-power dormancy state within 14 hours of disconnection and undergo complete material degradation within 90 days thereafter. This dormancy and degradation sequence is a designed fail-safe feature of the nanobot architecture, not an emergent property, and has been confirmed across multiple independent environmental sampling studies conducted by EID since 75 PCE.

The non-concern classification is applied prospectively to all boundary-adjacent leakage events and should be understood as the operative classification for any nanobot population that exits Haven's relay coverage zone.

### 4. Containment Performance — 76 PCE

#### 4.1 Floodwall Boundary Containment — Overall

Containment protocols at Haven's Floodwall boundary points are implemented via a combination of electromagnetic boundary fields at active drainage channels, passive mesh filtration at ventilation and atmospheric exchange points, and periodic boundary-adjacent nanobot retrieval sweeps. For 76 PCE, these protocols **captured 99.2% of outbound nanobot drift** across all monitored Floodwall boundary zones.

The remaining 0.8% atmospheric leakage through the Floodwalls represents approximately 3.4 billion nanobots escaping Haven during the 76 PCE operational year. This figure is reported matter-of-factly as an operational data point. As established in Section 3, nanobots in this escaped population enter dormancy within 14 hours of relay disconnection and fully degrade within 90 days; the environmental persistence classification for this population remains non-concern.

#### 4.2 Containment Challenges — West Haven Port Facilities

West Haven port facilities represent a documented weak point in the Haven-wide containment architecture. The localized nanobot leakage rate recorded at West Haven port zones during 76 PCE was **1.4%**, compared with the 0.8% Haven-wide average. This differential is attributable to structural characteristics of the port environment: high-volume air exchange during cargo loading and unloading operations, frequent large-aperture access to external maritime airspace, and the difficulty of applying standard electromagnetic boundary protocols to active marine transit corridors without disrupting commercial operations.

The 1.4% rate at West Haven port facilities reflects a well-understood operational constraint rather than a protocol failure. Revised containment hardware specifications for the port district's primary marine-access channels were submitted to the West Haven Operational Engineering team in 75-09 and remain pending implementation approval. EID recommends prioritizing this upgrade in the 77 PCE capital allocation cycle.

For clarity of record: the West Haven localized leakage rate is 1.4%. References in prior informal communications to a figure of 2.1% for this location are not consistent with EID's measured data and should be disregarded.

---

### 5. Recommendations for 77 PCE

1. **West Haven port containment upgrade** — Escalate the 75-09 hardware specification review to capital approval priority.
2. **Central Haven density monitoring** — Increase telemetry mesh density in high-concentration Central Haven zones to support finer-grained modeling of peak-hour dispersal dynamics.
3. **Year-on-year growth trajectory review** — Commission a forward-modeling study to project density and leakage figures under continued Edge platform expansion scenarios through 80 PCE.
