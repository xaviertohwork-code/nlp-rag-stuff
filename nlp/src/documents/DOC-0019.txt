---

**TO:** Senior Leadership Team
**FROM:** Park Soo-Hyun, Chief Executive Officer

**RE:** First 90-Day Strategic Review — CEO Assessment and Preliminary Restructuring Direction

---

This memo summarizes my assessment after ninety days in this role. I have no interest in softening what I found. The purpose of this document is to name the problems clearly so that we can begin solving them.

---

### I. CRITICAL PROBLEMS IDENTIFIED

During my first 90 days as CEO, I identified three critical problems requiring immediate structural attention: **operational cost bloat**, **absence of a digital-first content strategy**, and **accelerating talent attrition to competing firms**. These are not isolated issues. They are mutually reinforcing, and left unaddressed, any one of them is sufficient to prevent recovery of our market position. Together, they represent an existential risk to this organization.

---

### II. TALENT ATTRITION

The attrition figures are not acceptable. During the first 90 days of my tenure, Renhwa lost **740 employees** — a **6.5% quarterly attrition rate**. The departures are concentrated in creative production, digital distribution, and mid-level editorial management: precisely the competencies we most need to rebuild. Exit interviews, where conducted, consistently cite three factors — compensation below market rate, absence of a credible growth narrative, and the perception that this organization lacks a viable future.

This perception is not irrational. It is our job to change the underlying reality, not just the messaging.

Immediate action items: compensation benchmarking against current market rates, expedited review of retention packages for critical-path personnel, and a mandatory exit interview protocol for all departures above Grade 7.

---

### III. OPERATIONAL COST BLOAT

Renhwa's **content production costs are currently 2.3 times the industry average per unit of output**. I want that figure to sit with everyone reading this memo. Two point three times. We are producing content at a cost structure appropriate for a dominant market player with locked-in distribution channels. We are neither of those things.

A significant component of this bloat is our physical production infrastructure in east Haven. Our east Haven footprint was sized for the organization we were a decade ago. I am directing an immediate audit of all east Haven facility usage, with a target of **reducing our east Haven physical footprint by a further 15% by the end of fiscal year 74 PCE**. This is a floor, not a ceiling. Lease renegotiations, consolidations, and where appropriate, full facility exits should all be on the table.

---

### IV. DIGITAL-FIRST STRATEGY GAP

Renhwa does not have a digital-first content strategy. We have legacy distribution infrastructure, legacy production workflows, and legacy assumptions about how audiences consume content. Our competitors have spent the last decade building for the formats and channels that actually exist today. We have spent the last decade defending formats that existed fifteen years ago.

The absence of a credible digital-first strategy is not a secondary concern — it is the structural reason our cost base cannot be justified and our talent cannot be retained. Without a compelling answer to the question of what Renhwa is building toward, we cannot recruit, we cannot negotiate from strength, and we cannot make the case for our continued independence.

This is the hardest problem, and it will not be solved in the next quarter. What we can do in the next quarter is stop pretending we have a plan when we do not, and begin building one honestly.

---

### V. STRATEGIC OUTLOOK AND PRELIMINARY RESTRUCTURING DIRECTION

The restructuring plan I am initiating rests on three simultaneous tracks: cost reduction (as outlined above), talent stabilization, and distribution strategy renewal.

On distribution strategy, I want to be direct: organic rebuilding alone will not close the gap in any commercially relevant timeframe. I have begun preliminary conversations with **three external technology partners**, whose identities I am not disclosing in this memorandum — all three have been described to you here only by sector relevance — including a major immersive platform provider, a logistics-adjacent distribution network, and a data infrastructure firm with content-routing capabilities. These are early-stage conversations, not commitments. I raise them here solely to indicate that I am treating external partnership as a necessary component of any realistic recovery path, and that I am actively pursuing options.

The risks are real. External partnerships constrain editorial autonomy. They create dependencies. They will be opposed internally and externally. I am not unaware of this. I am also not aware of a costless alternative.

More detail on each track will follow in sector-specific briefings scheduled for the month of 73-10.
