# Personal Diary Extract
**Author:** Unnamed, Island Council Member, Nyari Confederation
**Recovered from:** Private residence, outer Confederation island
**Date:** 2113-11-02 CE
**Classification:** OPEN

**2113-11-02**

A grey morning, the kind where the sea and the sky blur into each other at the horizon. I walked the coastal path before the council session and felt, not for the first time this year, that the world is a much smaller and stranger place than it was even two or three years ago. The Cascade's children are still being born — whole communities still reshuffling themselves, finding new anchors wherever they can.

Which brings me to the group I have been meaning to write about for some weeks. Sometime in the past year — nobody seems to be able to say precisely when they first appeared, only that they seemed to arrive gradually, like a tide coming in — a new spiritual community has established itself here on the island. Perhaps thirty or forty people, by my rough estimate, though their numbers seem to shift. They keep to themselves in a cluster of rented properties near the old monitoring station on the eastern shore, and they are, I must say, thoroughly pleasant to encounter in the market or along the waterfront path.

What strikes me most, and what I find myself unable to entirely account for, is the way they address one another. I was introduced to three of them last week by a mutual acquaintance, and not once did I hear a proper name exchanged. They use numbered titles instead — one introduced himself as something like "the Fourteenth," and the others were referred to in similar fashion. My acquaintance explained it as a spiritual practice, a levelling of ego, which I suppose is not entirely without precedent. Still, it is unusual enough that I remarked on it to my wife that evening, and she laughed and called me a gossip.

I have had occasion to speak with them twice more since then. They are, without exception, peaceful, generous with their time, and entirely willing to discuss the weather, the fishing yields, the upcoming council resolutions on coastal zoning. But whenever the conversation edges toward what they actually believe — what animates the community, what they gather for, what their titles signify — they deflect with a gentleness that is almost elegant. They are exceptionally guarded about their beliefs in a way that I cannot quite dismiss, though I have tried.

One of the elder women on the council, Marete, flagged to me this week that the group has been making particular efforts to recruit among the younger cryptographers on the island — several of whom have recently completed their training rotations with the federal signals division. I will admit this gave me a moment's pause. Young people with technical skills, drawn to a community that values privacy and, evidently, its own internal codes. But then, young people find meaning in all manner of places, and cryptographers are not a uniquely impressionable group. I see no reason to escalate this to the security services or to formally register any concern with the council. To do so would feel like an overreach — these are, after all, private citizens practicing what appears to be a private faith.

I wrote a brief note in my personal log and left it at that. Harmless seekers finding meaning in troubled times, I told myself — and I believe it. Clairos has given people enough cause to seek comfort in community, in ritual, in whatever structure holds the dark at bay.

The zoning resolution can wait until Thursday.
